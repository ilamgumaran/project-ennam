# சோதனை-006 · உறவு மொழிகள் | The Kindred Comparison: Tamil · Japanese · Korean

**Date:** 2026-07-09 · **Question:** re-score on the **resonance axes** (`../appaal/README.md` §3) — not the philosophy axes of ஒப்பாய்வு — against the two languages typologically closest to Tamil's profile. **Integrity clause renewed:** concessions are scored where owed.

## §1 · Why these two

Japanese and Korean are Tamil's structural kin: agglutinative, SOV, postpositional, pro-drop, honorific-bearing, ideophone-rich, with celebrated reader-completion genres. (The old Tamil–Japanese/Korean *genetic* relationship theories — Ōno Susumu's Tamil-Japanese hypothesis — remain unaccepted by mainstream linguistics [F-lit]; the kinship here is **typological**, which is what matters for design.) If the resonance axes mean anything, this family is where Tamil must defend them.

## §2 · Axis by axis

**1 · Attention-order alignment.** All three scramble freely — case particles/beads free the word order to follow attention. Draw on flexibility. Tamil's distinction: **the verb carries person** (வந்தேன் — the dissolvable self leaves a trace in morphology); Japanese and Korean verbs mark politeness and aspect but *not person* — the dropped self is recoverable only from context. For a record-keeping project: **edge Tamil.**

**2 · Iconic channel width.** Conceded, both directions. Japanese **giongo/gitaigo** is the world's largest, most grammatically integrated ideophone system (thousands, productive, syntactically native). Korean adds what neither Tamil nor Japanese has: **generative iconicity** — systematic vowel-grading where light↔dark vowel alternation *modulates the felt intensity* of the same ideophone root (반짝 / 번쩍 — a twinkle vs a flash). Tamil's இரட்டைக்கிளவி is living and large; **JP wins width, KR wins system.** Lesson for phase 3: iconicity can be *engineered into a grading system* — Korean proves the channel is systematizable, not just collectable.

**3 · Trigger-forms (reader-completion).** The axis splits into the tension appaal §2 predicted. Japanese **haiku** with its **kireji** (cutting word — an engineered gap in the poem's syntax) is the world's most famous *open* completion form: minimal codebook, the reader arrives — but each reader arrives *somewhere of their own*. Tamil **உள்ளுறை** is *coded* completion: the thinai codebook makes the arrival **deterministic** — lossless, but tuition-priced. Korean **sijo**'s third-line twist sits between. No winner — a **design axis discovered**: *open gap = low learning, variable arrival; coded gap = lossless, costly learning.* Phase 3's medium wants the unclaimed corner: **a gap that is coded by a generative (self-teaching) codebook.**

**4 · Generative core.** **Hangul wins this axis outright, against everyone.** Created deliberately (1443–46, Sejong's commission), it is a **featural script**: the letters are diagrams of the mouth — ㄱ the tongue root closing the velum, ㄴ the tongue tip at the ridge, ㅁ the lips, ㅅ the teeth; add a stroke for aspiration, double for tension. The learner doesn't memorize letters; the letters *show their own pronunciation*. Result: literacy famously acquirable in a day — history's greatest single act of medium-engineering, and a working **proof-of-concept of appaal principles 3–4** (encode in the body's dimensions; structure that teaches itself). Tamil's 31→247 generativity is strong (and its புள்ளி was real engineering); Japanese kana are compact but **kanji is the anti-generative counterexample** — thousands of listed, underivable symbols. Ranking: **KR ≫ TA > JP.**
And the bridge that matters most to us: **தொல்காப்பியம் described the articulators in text (பிறப்பியல்); Sejong drew them into letterforms.** Tamil owns the described map; Korean demonstrated the featural method. Phase 3 inherits both halves.

**5 · Interference profile (rank conjugated into speech).** Korean grammaticalizes **six-plus speech levels**, mandatory on every sentence-final verb; Japanese **keigo** runs parallel honorific/humble verb paradigms. Tamil's rank-machinery (நீ/நீங்கள், -ஆர்) is real (எ-004 stands) but is the **lightest of the three**. For a medium that shouldn't force social judgment through every utterance: **edge Tamil.**

**6 · Two-substrate viability.** Both kin are **high-resource**: vast corpora, mature tooling, strong tokenizer support; Korean's syllable-block Unicode is byte-efficient; Japanese's kanji density partially offsets its script mix. Tamil currently trails on resources (எ-007/எ-008) — but the kin prove the ceiling: **languages of this exact typological shape run at full machine-scale today.** Tamil's gap is circumstance, not structure — now demonstrated by family precedent.

## §4 · What each kin teaches Ennam

- **From Japanese:** the iconic ceiling — how wide the sound-meaning channel can go when a culture *keeps* it; and the art of the open gap (kireji as engineered incompleteness).
- **From Korean:** the two most actionable lessons in this whole comparative program: **(i) featural encoding works** — meaning-carriers can be shaped like the body that produces them, collapsing learning cost (appaal principles 3–4, historically proven); **(ii) iconicity can be graded** — a small systematic alternation multiplying expressive precision without new vocabulary.
- **Tamil keeps:** coded lossless completion (உள்ளுறை), the self's trace in the verb, the lightest honorific tax of the family, the deepest living-classical line — plus the mission/pair grounds of ஒப்பாய்வு §7, none of which this comparison weakened.

The working line updates: **mint like Sanskrit, wire like Greek, ideophone like Japanese, engineer like Hangul, close like the kural, live like Tamil.**
