# மொழி வரலாறு | How the Language Evolved

The story of Tamil as a living system — what changed, what refused to change, and when. Companion file: `arivu-varalaru.md` (how the *knowledge about* Tamil was built, and what biases entered with it). Dates are given with their honest uncertainty; contested claims are marked **[contested]**.

## 1. வேர் | The Root

Tamil belongs to the **Dravidian family** — a family unrelated to Sanskrit and the Indo-European languages, with its own reconstructed ancestor (Proto-Dravidian, likely spoken ~2500–1000 BCE; reconstruction, not record). Tamil sits in the southern branch, and among all Dravidian languages it is the earliest and most fully recorded. Malayalam is its closest sibling — a western dialect continuum that grew apart roughly between the 9th and 13th centuries CE.

Two facts anchor everything else. First, Tamil is not derived from Sanskrit; where the two share words, the traffic ran in both directions. Second, Tamil is the rare case of a classical language that never died — the language of the oldest poems and the language of today's kitchen are recognizably one system, changed but unbroken.

## 2. கல்வெட்டு | The Evidence Line

The earliest physical Tamil is written in **தமிழ்-பிராமி (Tamil-Brahmi)** — the Brahmi script adapted for Tamil sounds, scratched on cave beds, pots, and coins.

- Conventional dating of the earliest inscriptions: **~3rd–2nd century BCE** (Mangulam, Jambai, and dozens of cave sites).
- The **கீழடி (Keezhadi)** excavations near Madurai (from 2014) produced a carbon-dated sample of **580 BCE** from the settlement, and the Tamil Nadu Department of Archaeology claims Tamil-Brahmi potsherds from the 6th century BCE **[contested]** — critics note the dated samples and the inscribed sherds are not securely from the same layer. What is not contested: Keezhadi shows a literate, urban, brick-built society on the Vaigai well before the common era.
- Tamil-Brahmi contains a genuine engineering act: the **புள்ளி** (the dot marking a bare consonant) — an innovation no other Brahmi descendant made. The very first written Tamil already shows deliberate adaptation of a borrowed tool to Tamil's own phonology. The script was engineered from day one.

Old Tamil words also traveled with trade — proposed loans into Greek and Hebrew (e.g., அரிசி → *oryza*, இஞ்சி வேர் → *zingiber*, தோகை → *tukki*) are debated in detail but collectively point to Tamil in Indian Ocean commerce by the early centuries BCE.

## 3. பழந்தமிழ் | Old Tamil (~300 BCE – 700 CE)

The language of the Sangam corpus (see `../ilakkiyam/`) and of Tolkappiyam. If you read a Sangam poem today you are reading Old Tamil — hard, but not foreign. Its signature features:

- **Two tenses, not three.** Old Tamil verbs marked *past* and *non-past*; the dedicated present (-கிறு-) is a later, Middle Tamil development. The classical mind divided time into "done" and "not done."
- **ஆய்தம் (ஃ) was alive**, a real sound in real words, not the fossil it is now.
- **அளபெடை** — vowels stretched beyond their length for meter and feeling (படூஉம், தலைஇ) — written into the poems themselves: duration as meaning.
- **Very few Sanskrit loans.** The Sangam corpus is strikingly self-sufficient in vocabulary; Prakrit/Sanskrit items exist but are thin on the ground.
- Already fully **agglutinative and verb-final** — the deep syntax you speak today was in place two thousand years ago.

## 4. இடைக்காலத் தமிழ் | Middle Tamil (~700 – 1600 CE)

The centuries of the bhakti movement, the imperial Cholas, and the great commentaries. The language:

- **Gained a present tense** (-கிறு-/-கின்று-), completing the three-way tense system.
- **Absorbed Sanskrit heavily** — temple, court, and philosophical registers filled with loans; a hybrid prestige style (மணிப்பிரவாளம், "gems and coral": Tamil and Sanskrit interleaved) flourished in some circles. The lexicon's religious and abstract layers still carry this era's imprint.
- **Changed script.** Early medieval Tamil country wrote in **வட்டெழுத்து** ("round script"); from the Pallava-Chola line came the letterforms that evolved into the modern Tamil script (11th century onward, stabilizing thereafter). **கிரந்தம் (Grantha)** — a parallel script for Sanskrit — supplied the letters ஜ ஷ ஸ ஹ க்ஷ that still handle loanwords.
- **Was codified again**: நன்னூல் (13th c.) became the standard school grammar, as it largely remains — a medieval lens through which the classical language is still taught.
- Chola-era Tamil became an **administrative world language** — inscriptions from the Ganges delta to Sumatra; tens of thousands of stone inscriptions in Tamil Nadu date from this age (Tamil has one of the largest epigraphic corpora on earth).

## 5. அச்சுத் தமிழ் | Print and Early Modern Tamil (1578 – 1900)

- **1578**: *தம்பிரான் வணக்கம்* (Thambiran Vanakkam), printed at Quilon by the missionary Henrique Henriques — **the first printed book in any Indian language or script.** Tamil met movable type before most of the world's languages.
- **1716**: Ziegenbalg's *Grammatica Damulica* — Tamil grammar for Europeans; dictionaries follow (Fabricius 1779).
- **வீரமாமுனிவர் (Beschi, 18th c.)** reformed the orthography: he systematized the distinction between short and long **எ/ஏ** and **ஒ/ஓ**, which earlier writing left ambiguous. That a reader today can see vowel length at a glance is his doing — evolution by deliberate act, again.
- **Prose is born late.** Classical Tamil's genius ran to verse and to commentary; sustained modern prose emerges in this era — Beschi's satire *பரமார்த்த குருவின் கதை*, 19th-century essayists (ஆறுமுக நாவலர்), and the **first Tamil novel, பிரதாப முதலியார் சரித்திரம் (1879)**.
- The palm-leaf classics nearly died and were rescued in one generation — that story belongs to `arivu-varalaru.md` §7.

## 6. இக்காலத் தமிழ் | Modern Tamil (1900 – now)

- **தனித்தமிழ் இயக்கம்** (Pure Tamil movement, மறைமலை அடிகள், 1916): a deliberate de-Sanskritization of the lexicon. Thousands of coinages replaced loans; the movement reshaped formal Tamil and proved, again, that the language accepts engineering — while raising its own bias questions (see `arivu-varalaru.md` §8).
- **Language politics**: anti-Hindi agitations (1937–40, 1965) made Tamil identity a political force; language became the axis of Tamil Nadu's public life.
- **1978**: Government of Tamil Nadu script standardization — irregular ligatures of ஆ, ஒ, ஓ, ஐ combinations regularized (ணா, றா, னா, ணை, லை, ளை written with the standard signs). The alphabet you drill in பாடம் 01 is partly a 1978 artifact.
- **2004**: Tamil becomes the **first legally recognized classical language of India**.
- **Digital Tamil**: Unicode from 1991; a state-backed coinage culture (கணினி *computer*, மென்பொருள் *software*, இணையம் *internet* — successful engineered words in daily use); a global diaspora (Sri Lanka's war scattering Tamil across every continent) writing the language everywhere.
- **Now**: Tamil is spoken by ~80+ million people, official in two countries (India — state level — and Sri Lanka, plus Singapore), and is learning to live inside machine intelligence — which is where this repo stands.

## 7. மாறியது · மாறாதது | What Changed, What Refused

| Changed | Refused to change |
|---|---|
| Tense system (2 → 3) | Verb-final, left-branching syntax |
| Lexicon (Sanskrit in, then partly out; English now) | Agglutinative word-building |
| Script (Tamil-Brahmi → vatteluttu → Chola → 1978 forms) | The 12 + 18 + ஃ phoneme architecture |
| ஆய்தம் from sound to relic | Retroflex ழ — the sound in the language's own name |
| Prose: none → dominant | குறில்/நெடில் length distinction carrying meaning |
| Registers: diglossia deepened | The thinai vocabulary of landscape and feeling — still legible |

The pattern across 2,300 years: **the skeleton holds; the skin renews.** Deep structure (phonology, morphology, syntax) is astonishingly conservative; vocabulary and script have been repeatedly and *deliberately* re-engineered — by the pulli-makers, by Beschi, by the purists, by the 1978 committee, by the கணினி coiners.

## 8. எண்ணத்திற்கு | What This History Means for Ennam

Phase 2 of this project — evolving Tamil deliberately — is not a rupture. It is the **most traditional thing one can do in Tamil**: every era's Tamil was somebody's engineering project, argued over and absorbed. The history also warns: every past engineering carried its engineers' biases in with it. The audit of those biases is the next file.
