# அறிவு வரலாறு | How Tamil Was Built as Knowledge

The language is one thing; the *knowledge about* the language — what got written down, taught, preserved, canonized, and called "correct" — is another. Every layer of that knowledge had gatekeepers, and every gatekeeper had pressures. This file traces the layers so that later, when we probe Tamil for bias (phase 2), we can distinguish **bias in the record** (what survived and who chose it) from **bias in the language itself** (worldview baked into grammar). The ledger is at §9–10.

## 1. சங்கம் — the legend and the evidence

The tradition says three **சங்கங்கள்** (academies) sat at Madurai across thousands of years, the first two swallowed by the sea. This is **legend** — the flood-lost-continent elaborations (Lemuria/குமரிக்கண்டம்) are 19th–20th century accretions, not ancient memory. The evidence says: poems composed roughly **300 BCE – 300 CE** by hundreds of poets across the Tamil country, **compiled into anthologies centuries later**, with colophons (poet, patron, situation) added by compilers.

Why it matters: the corpus reaches us pre-filtered. Somebody chose which poems entered the eight anthologies; somebody attached the interpretive frame. The choosing hand is invisible, and that is the first bias gate.

## 2. தொல்காப்பியம் — the founding act

The oldest surviving Tamil grammar, and one of the oldest grammars of any living language. Dating is honestly uncertain: a defensible range puts the **core at ~2nd–1st century BCE** with **final redaction by ~5th century CE** [scholars disagree in both directions; Tamil tradition claims far older].

Its structure is the tradition's deepest signature: three books —

1. **எழுத்ததிகாரம்** — sounds and letters
2. **சொல்லதிகாரம்** — words and morphology
3. **பொருளதிகாரம்** — *content*: love, war, landscape, feeling, the situations of life and how poetry may speak them

Book three is the extraordinary one. No Greek, Latin, or Sanskrit grammar includes *the subject matter of experience* inside grammar. Tolkappiyam treats **how to express lived interiority as a rule-governed system** — the திணை landscapes, the permitted speakers, the moments of love and war that may be voiced. The tradition's founding text already believed what Ennam believes: **expression of experience is part of the language's engineering, not outside it.** (Logged as seed வி-005.)

The same book is also the first bias gate we can actually read: it codifies **who may speak** (which persons in which situations), gendered scripts (கற்பு chastity ideology enters the poetics), and social norms presented as grammar. The founding gift and the founding bias are the same page.

## 3. ஓலைச்சுவடி — the palm-leaf economy

Until print, Tamil survived by hand-copying onto palm leaves that rot in the southern climate within decades to a century or two. **Whatever was not recopied, died.** Copying was expensive, so the filter was: texts used in teaching, worship, and courtly prestige got copied; everything else drifted toward extinction.

Consequences we can measure: of the classical கீழ்க்கணக்கு and epics, works are *known by name but lost* (வளையாபதி nearly whole, குண்டலகேசி — both associated with Buddhist/Jain communities). The Kural survived in hundreds of copies; the Sangam anthologies survived in a *handful*. Survival tracked institutional favor, not merit. This is the record's largest single bias, and it is invisible precisely because the missing texts are missing.

## 4. மறை-வழி தொகுப்பு | Canonization by religion

Between the 10th–12th centuries the bhakti corpus was gathered into scripture: the Shaiva **திருமுறை** (twelve books, compiled by நம்பியாண்டார் நம்பி and successors) and the Vaishnava **நாலாயிர திவ்வியப் பிரபந்தம்** (compiled by நாதமுனி). Magnificent preservation — and a sectarian filter: what fit the canons was maintained by temple economies; Jain and Buddhist Tamil, once a major stream (சிலப்பதிகாரம்'s milieu, மணிமேகலை, the lost epics), fell outside the machinery and thinned to fragments.

## 5. உரை — commentary as knowledge-making

Tamil transmitted understanding through **உரை** (commentary): இளம்பூரணர், சேனாவரையர், நச்சினார்க்கினியர் on the grammars; **பரிமேலழகர்** (13th–14th c.) on the Kural. Commentators decided what texts *meant* — and their frames became the default reading for centuries. Parimelazhagar read the Kural through a Sanskritic-brahminical lens (அறம்/பொருள்/இன்பம் mapped to dharma-artha-kama, hierarchy read in); modern Dravidian-movement commentators read the same verses back out. **The text held still; the knowledge about it swung with each commentator's world.** Any bias probe must therefore always ask: is this in the text, or in its most famous commentary?

## 6. காலனிய மடிப்பு | The colonial refraction

Missionaries and colonial scholars built the modern *apparatus* — dictionaries (Fabricius 1779; the Madras University Tamil Lexicon 1924–36), printed grammars (from Ziegenbalg 1716), teaching institutions (College of Fort St George). Two distortions came with the gifts:

- **Category import**: Tamil grammar described through Latin and Sanskrit grammatical categories — case systems, parts of speech — that fit imperfectly; some of what we now "know" about Tamil grammar is a European template laid over it. (நன்னூல் had already laid a Sanskrit-shaped template over தொல்காப்பியம்; the colonial layer doubled it.)
- **Race entanglement**: Robert Caldwell's *Comparative Grammar* (1856) established the Dravidian family — sound linguistics — but the "Dravidian" concept immediately fused with 19th-century race theory. Language ≠ ancestry, but the fusion powered politics for a century and still colors what people believe the word "Tamil" names.

## 7. மீட்பு நெருக்கடி | The rescue bottleneck

By the mid-19th century the Sangam classics were **functionally lost** — unread, uncopied, unknown to most pandits, whose curriculum was grammar, Kural, and devotional texts. Two men — **சி. வை. தாமோதரம் பிள்ளை** and above all **உ. வே. சாமிநாதையர்** — spent decades hunting palm-leaf manuscripts village by village (சீவக சிந்தாமணி printed 1887, பத்துப்பாட்டு 1889, புறநானூறு 1894…). U.V.S.'s autobiography records manuscripts found as linings of storage boxes, eaten by ants, burned as fuel.

Stop on this: **the entire classical corpus passed through a bottleneck of two lifetimes.** What they found, we have; what they missed, we do not know we ever had. The canon's completeness is an illusion of survivorship. (And the lesson for this repo is direct — முறை §7, keep the record. A civilization nearly lost its deepest self for want of recopying.)

## 8. மறுமலர்ச்சியும் தூய்மையும் | Renaissance and purism

The rescued classics detonated in a culture mid-awakening. The **தனித்தமிழ் இயக்கம்** (Pure Tamil movement, from 1916) and the Dravidian movement rebuilt formal Tamil, purging Sanskrit loans, coining native replacements, tying language to identity and politics. Real achievements — and a new filter: wordstock became ideology. A Sanskrit-derived word in a Tamil sentence now carries a *position*; so does its தனித்தமிழ் replacement. The lexicon is politically charged in both directions, and neutral word-choice is sometimes simply unavailable. That too is a structure a thought must pass through.

## 9. பதிவின் சார்புகள் | The Bias Ledger — biases in the record

| Era | Gatekeeper | Selection pressure | Trace in today's Tamil |
|---|---|---|---|
| Anthology compilation | Court-adjacent compilers | Patronage; praise economy (புறம் poems literally paid for) | Heroic/royal frame over ordinary life; colophon framings read as fact |
| தொல்காப்பியம் codification | Grammarian elite | Norms presented as rules | Gender scripts (கற்பு), who-may-speak conventions inherited as "grammar" |
| Terminology itself | Grammarians | செந்தமிழ் ("refined") vs கொடுந்தமிழ் ("crooked") | Value judgment on registers baked into the words for them; feeds diglossia (எ-003) |
| Palm-leaf copying | Teachers, temples | Copy-or-perish; institutional use | Jain/Buddhist stream thinned; Kural everywhere, Sangam nearly lost |
| Bhakti canonization | Sectarian compilers | Fit-to-canon | Religious survival filter on medieval literature |
| Commentary | உரையாசிரியர்கள் | Each commentator's worldview | Default meanings (esp. Kural via பரிமேலழகர்) that may not be the text's |
| Colonial scholarship | Missionaries, philologists | European categories; race science | Grammar described in borrowed frames; "Dravidian" fused with ancestry |
| Rescue | Two scholars' routes and luck | Whatever survived to be found | Canon = survivorship, mistaken for completeness |
| Purism | Movement intellectuals | De-Sanskritization | Politicized lexicon; no neutral register in some domains |

## 10. மொழியுள் சார்புகள் | Biases inside the language itself

Different in kind — not about what survived, but about what the grammar *makes speakers do*:

- **உயர்திணை / அஃறிணை**: every noun must be classed as "high-kind" (humans, deities) or "not-high-kind" (everything else — animals, things, ideas). The classification is mandatory and value-named. A sentence cannot be neutral about it. (Logged as எ-004.)
- **Gendered verb endings**: finite verbs mark speaker/subject gender in the third person (வந்தான்/வந்தாள்) — gender assignment is grammatically forced where English can stay silent.
- **Honorific grammar**: நீ/நீங்கள், வா/வாங்க, singular/plural-as-respect — social rank is conjugated. You cannot address someone without positioning them.
- **And the counterweight**: the same grammar gives நாம்/நாங்கள் clusivity (வி-003) — a *precision about relationship* English lacks. Grammatical worldview cuts both ways; the probe must stay honest about the gifts.

## 11. முறை குறிப்பு | Method note for the later audit

When phase 2 tests any bias claim: (1) locate it — text, commentary, record, or grammar; (2) check against usage, not just prescription (corpora, speech); (3) the native ear rules on felt weight (முறை §8); (4) F-claims only — "the grammar forces X" is checkable; "Tamils therefore think Y" is not, and we do not assert it.
