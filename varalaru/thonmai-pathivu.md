# தொன்மைப் பதிவு | The Ancient Record

What ancient Tamil actually left behind — stone, pot, coin, and the testimony of foreigners — as distinct from literature copied and recopied through later hands. Two evidence streams with different reliability: **the dug-up record** (dated by context, unedited by tradition) and **the transmitted record** (the corpus — richer, but filtered; see `arivu-varalaru.md`). This file walks the dug-up stream, then shows where the two converge.

## 1. கற்பதிவு | The Stones at Home

- **Cave inscriptions**: 90+ sites, thickest around Madurai — rock shelters cut for Jain ascetics, their drip-ledges carved in **Tamil-Brahmi**, conventionally ~3rd–2nd century BCE onward. **மாங்குளம் (Mangulam)** is the anchor: its inscriptions (~2nd c. BCE) name a பாண்டியன் நெடுஞ்செழியன் — a Sangam-corpus royal name, on stone, in the corpus's own era.
- What the inscriptions *are* matters as much as their date: overwhelmingly **donation records** — a merchant, a salt-dealer, a gold-worker, a village gifting a monk's stone bed. The earliest written Tamil is the language of **givers naming themselves**.
- **நடுகல் (hero stones)**: memorials to the fallen cattle-raid dead — and தொல்காப்பியம் itself codifies the hero-stone ritual in stages (காட்சி to வாழ்த்து): the grammar book documents the stone culture the archaeologists dig up. Textbook and trowel agree.

## 2. பானை எழுத்து | The Pots

Pottery graffiti — names and short words scratched on ordinary cooking and storage vessels — at கொடுமணல் (weaver-jeweler industrial site), அழகன்குளம் (port), கீழடி (urban settlement; the contested 6th-c.-BCE claims live here — see `mozhi-varalaru.md` §2), and dozens more.

This is the record's quiet bombshell: **ordinary people wrote.** Not scribes, not priests — potters, traders, householders scratching *their own names* on *their own pots*. Ancient Tamil literacy, as far as the ground shows it, was **civic and commercial, not clerical**. No temple monopoly, no scribal caste gatekeeping the letters. (For the bias ledger: the *record-keeping* was democratic; the *anthology-keeping* centuries later was not.)

Add the **coins** — Sangam-age சேர/சோழ/பாண்டிய issues with Tamil-Brahmi legends — and the picture holds: writing lived in the economy.

## 3. உலகின் சாட்சி | The World's Testimony

The earliest *dated* appearances of Tamil polities are in other people's records:

- **~260 BCE — Ashoka's Rock Edicts II & XIII**: the Mauryan emperor lists his southern neighbors *beyond his domain* — **சோழ, பாண்டிய, சத்தியபுத்திர, கேரளபுத்திர** — as sovereign states he sends physicians and envoys to, not provinces. When empire looked south, it saw the three crowns already standing.
- **~2nd–1st c. BCE — Hathigumpha inscription** (Kalinga): King Kharavela boasts of breaking a **த்ரமிர confederacy** (Tramira-desa-sanghata) — a Tamil league old and organized enough to be *worth boasting about*, over a thousand kilometers north.
- **~1st c. CE — the Periplus of the Erythraean Sea**, then Ptolemy: Greek merchant-geography of Tamil ports (முசிறி, கொற்கை…), pepper, pearls, fine cloth; corroborated by Roman coin hoards across Tamil country and the Arikamedu excavations.
- **Egypt, the Red Sea ports**: Tamil-Brahmi sherds at **Quseir al-Qadim** and **Berenike** (1st c. BCE–1st c. CE). One reads **பானை ஒறி** — "pot in a rope-sling." A Tamil trader's shipping label, on the African coast, twenty centuries old, still legible to Ilam today.
- **Thailand — Phu Khao Thong**: Tamil-Brahmi on pottery (early centuries CE), the eastern arm of the same network.

Note what carried the language outward: **not conquest — cargo.** Tamil's first international appearances are a medical-aid treaty listing, a trade league, and shipping labels.

## 4. காலக்கூடம் | How the Corpus Gets Its Dates

The transmitted literature is dated by triangulating: the **Gajabahu synchronism** (சிலப்பதிகாரம்'s சேரன் செங்குட்டுவன் hosting Lanka's Gajabahu, ~2nd c. CE — the classic anchor, used with due caution); corpus-internal cross-references (poets addressing kings named on stone, as at Mangulam); the **பதிற்றுப்பத்து**'s structured royal decades; and language stages. Convergent result: composition roughly **300 BCE – 300 CE**, the same window as the inscriptions, coins, and foreign testimony. The two evidence streams — dug-up and transmitted — describe one world: the corpus's cattle raids, ports, pearl fisheries, and gift-economy match the stones and sherds item for item.

## 5. தொகுப்பு | What the Ancient Record Establishes

1. **Literacy by the 3rd–2nd century BCE** at latest (earlier claims contested — Keezhadi), in a script already engineered for Tamil (the புள்ளி).
2. **A broad literate public**: merchants, artisans, ascetics' patrons, pot-owners — writing entered Tamil life through the market and the gift, not the altar.
3. **Statehood older than its first dating**: by 260 BCE the three crowns were fixtures worth an emperor's diplomacy; by Kharavela's day, capable of confederacy.
4. **A trade language with global reach**: Egypt to Thailand, labels and names — Tamil's ancient internationalism was commercial and voluntary.
5. **Text and ground agree**: the grammar codifies the hero-stone; the poems sing the ports the Greeks mapped; the kings on colophons appear on cave walls. The corpus is not a later fantasy of antiquity — it is antiquity, transmitted.

## 6. எண்ணத்திற்கு | For Ennam

The ancient record's texture repeats a finding from `inru-thamizh.md` in reverse: Tamil has *always* lived in the channels ordinary people actually use — pots then, feeds now. The language never passed through a scribal-priesthood bottleneck that phase 2 would now have to break; its oldest habit is **everyone writes**. And the first written Tamil sentence-fragments are acts of relation — gifts recorded, goods named for a counterparty across the sea. The medium enters history already doing what this project asks of it: carrying meaning faithfully between parties who must trust the carrying.
