# பாடம் 03 · வினை: கடைசியில் வரும் உண்மை
# Lesson 03 · The Verb: Truth Arrives Last

Prerequisite: பாடம் 02 (beads). The verb is Tamil's densest bead-work — and where the sentence's whole design shows.

## 1. The sentence skeleton: hold your breath till the end

Tamil is **verb-final** (Subject–Object–Verb):

> நான் புத்தகம் படித்தேன். — I book read.

Everything else can move for emphasis; the verb holds the last position, and with it the tense, the person, and the sentence's fate. An English listener knows the action early and the details later; a Tamil listener holds all the pieces **open** until the final word closes them. Feel this in your own speech — the sentence is suspended, then *lands*.

## 2. The verb is itself a bead-string

**வினை = வேர் + காலம் + ஆள்** (root + tense + person):

> வா (come) → வ + ந்த் + ஏன் → **வந்தேன்** (come-past-I: I came)
> வ + ருகிற் + ஏன் → **வருகிறேன்** (I am coming)
> வ + ருவ் + ஏன் → **வருவேன்** (I will come)

Same three beads every time: root, tense marker (ந்த்/கிற்/வ் families), person ending.

## 3. The ending carries a whole census

Third-person endings mark gender, number, politeness, and திணை — the பாடம் 02 worldview, now conjugated:

| | வந்த்- + | meaning |
|---|---|---|
| I | -ஏன் → வந்தேன் | I came |
| you | -ஆய் → வந்தாய் | you came (intimate) |
| he | -ஆன் → வந்தான் | he came (plain) |
| she | -ஆள் → வந்தாள் | she came (plain) |
| he/she honored | -ஆர் → வந்தார் | came (respect) |
| it (அஃறிணை) | -அது → வந்தது | it came |
| they (people) | -ஆர்கள் → வந்தார்கள் | they came |

Two things hide in this table. **Gender is forced** in plain 3rd person — you cannot say "came" of a person without declaring he/she/honored. And **rank is grammatical**: வந்தான் vs வந்தார் positions the person socially; there is no unpositioned option. (Both flagged: `../varalaru/arivu-varalaru.md` §10.) The honored -ஆர் is also the everyday escape hatch: when in doubt, honor.

## 4. The self can vanish — the verb keeps it

Because the ending carries the person, the pronoun may drop:

> **வந்தேன்.** — full sentence: "I came."

One word holding actor, action, and time. Tamil sentences routinely run without pronouns; the verb *is* the self-record. (For the research track: compression again — and a mirror of the family's essays, where the "I" dissolves and only the act's trace remains. → `saaram.md` §4.)

## 5. Being without "is"

For plain identity, Tamil uses **no verb at all**:

> அவன் மாணவன். — he (is a) student.
> இது வீடு. — this (is a) house.

Two nouns set side by side; being is asserted by *arrangement*, not by a word. English cannot say anything without "is"; Tamil finds "is" unnecessary for what simply *is*. Existence/location gets இரு (அவன் வீட்டில் இருக்கிறான்), but identity is zero-marked.

## 6. Saying no is its own system

> வரவில்லை — didn't come / isn't coming (tense dissolves in negation!)
> வரமாட்டேன் — I will not come (refusal)
> வராதே — don't come!

Note the first: **negation collapses past and present into one form.** The classical two-tense mind (பாடம் நோக்கு: `../varalaru/mozhi-varalaru.md` §3) survives inside the modern negative.

## 7. பயிற்சி | Drills

1. **Ten daily verbs** (வா, போ, சாப்பிடு, பார், கேள், சொல், படி, எழுது, தூங்கு, சிரி): conjugate each for நான்/நீ/அவன்/அவள்/அவர், past tense, aloud, then written.
2. **Pronoun-drop diary.** Each night, three sentences about your day with *no pronouns* — verb endings only.
3. **Zero-copula hunt.** Write 5 identity sentences (X Y.) about your room. Feel the missing "is."
4. **The politeness ladder.** Take வா: வா / வாரும் / வாங்க(ள்). Say each imagining the person you'd use it with. Journal who appeared — that's grammar drawing your social map.

## 8. நூல் | Closing text — Bharathi

> யாமறிந்த மொழிகளிலே தமிழ்மொழி போல்
> இனிதாவது எங்கும் காணோம்.

*In all the tongues we know, we find none so sweet as Tamil* — பாரதியார், the voice that carried Tamil into the modern age. Note his verbs: அறிந்த (bead it apart), காணோம் — "we do not see": the classical **-ஓம்** we-ending, negation and tense fused, poetry running on pure grammar.
