# திருக்குறள் | The Kural: Seven Seas in a Mustard Seed

**திருவள்ளுவர்** · likely ~450–500 CE (traditional dating much earlier; scholarly ranges vary) · 1,330 couplets in 133 chapters of 10, across three books: **அறம்** (right living), **பொருள்** (wealth/statecraft), **இன்பம்** (love). The most copied, translated, quoted Tamil text — the one work the palm-leaf filter never endangered.

## 1. The form is the philosophy

A **குறள் வெண்பா** is two lines: four சீர் (metrical words) then three — seven units total, roughly a dozen words, governed by strict venba rules. The traditional praise: வள்ளுவர் *drilled the mustard seed and poured in the seven seas.* The form forces a discipline our project should study: **whatever cannot be compressed into seven சீர் without loss has not yet been fully realized.** Compression as a *test* of realization — not a limit on it.

## 2. Three kurals, read closely

### குறள் 391 — on learning (fits this repo's hand)

> கற்க கசடறக் கற்பவை கற்றபின்
> நிற்க அதற்குத் தக.

*Learn flawlessly what is worth learning; then **stand** in what you learned — live to its measure.*

Two imperatives frame the couplet: கற்க (learn) opens, நிற்க (stand) pivots the second line. Learning that does not become standing — conduct, posture — is disqualified as learning. The word கசடு is dross, slag: learning has impurities to be smelted out, not just gaps to be filled. Our பாடம் track answers to this verse.

### குறள் 80 — on love (and the soul-body pattern again)

> அன்பின் வழியது உயிர்நிலை அஃதிலார்க்கு
> என்புதோல் போர்த்த உடம்பு.

*The seat of life rides on the way of love; those without it are bones wrapped in skin — a body only.*

The script's own metaphysics (உயிர் soul / மெய் body — பாடம் 01, வி-002) reappears as ethics: love is the உயிர் of the person; without it only the மெய் remains — and the poem's word for the loveless body is brutal anatomy: என்புதோல், bone-and-skin. Note ஃ alive in classical use (அஃதிலார்க்கு). One conceptual pattern — soul-animates-body — running through alphabet, ethics, and poetics: languages carry *load-bearing metaphors*, and this is Tamil's deepest one.

### குறள் 397 — the Kural answers யாதும் ஊரே

> யாதானும் நாடாமால் ஊராமால் என்னொருவன்
> சாந்துணையும் கல்லாத வாறு.

*To the learned, every country is their country, every town their town — how then does one stay unlearned till death?*

The famous Sangam claim — யாதும் ஊரே (Puram 192) — reappears with a mechanism attached: **learning** is what makes everywhere home. Where the Puram poem grounds boundarylessness in metaphysics (the raft on the river), the Kural grounds it in cultivation. Two answers to one question, four centuries apart, both canonical.

## 3. What the Kural teaches Ennam

Three findings for the pattern file. **Compression-as-test** (§1 — logged வி-008). **Load-bearing metaphor**: உயிர்/மெய் structures alphabet, ethics, poetics alike — evolving Tamil means respecting, or consciously extending, its deep metaphors. **Reader-completion**: a kural states 90% and the reader closes the arc; the form *requires* an active mind — meaning is co-produced, which is the resonance structure again, this time between text and reader.

And one caution from the bias ledger (§5 of `../varalaru/arivu-varalaru.md`): what most readers "know" the Kural means is often பரிமேலழகர்'s 13th-century frame. Reading the bare text against its commentaries is itself a bias-detection exercise — one we will do.
